Categories: mathematics calculus integration difference-equations

Building Calculus from First Principles

Article VIII: Difference Equations for the Anti-Derivative

Preface

In our series Building Calculus from First Principles, we have focused on expressing functions as sums of infinitesimal contributions and revealing their underlying algebraic patterns. In this article, we turn our attention to the difference equations that connect a function with its antiderivative. Instead of relying solely on retrospectively analyzing known formulas, we compile a set of difference equations which, by “peeling off” the increments, reveal the pattern behind the antiderivative. Our method is entirely elementary and algebraic, emphasizing clear, step-by-step reasoning.

1. Difference Equations for Powers

The core idea is to relate the differential expression xp  dxx^p\;dx to the difference between consecutive powers of xx. For very small dxdx (or in the limit as dxdx becomes infinitesimal), we have the following difference equations:

x  dx=(x+dx)2−x22,x2  dx=(x+dx)3−x33,x3  dx=(x+dx)4−x44,x4  dx=(x+dx)5−x55.\begin{array}{ll} x\; dx = \frac{(x + dx)^2 - x^2}{2},\\[1mm] x^2\; dx = \frac{(x + dx)^3 - x^3}{3},\\[1mm] x^3\; dx = \frac{(x + dx)^4 - x^4}{4},\\[1mm] x^4\; dx = \frac{(x + dx)^5 - x^5}{5}. \end{array}

Explanation

  • For the first equation:Expand (x+dx)2(x + dx)^2:
(x+dx)2=x2+2x  dx+(dx)2.(x + dx)^2 = x^2 + 2x\;dx + (dx)^2.

Neglecting the (dx)2(dx)^2 term (since dxdx is infinitesimally small), we obtain:

(x+dx)2−x2≈2x  dx.(x + dx)^2 - x^2 \approx 2x\;dx.

Dividing by 2 yields:

x  dx≈(x+dx)2−x22.x\;dx \approx \frac{(x + dx)^2 - x^2}{2}.
  • For the second equation:Similarly, expand (x+dx)3(x+dx)^3:
(x+dx)3=x3+3x2  dx+3x  (dx)2+(dx)3.(x+dx)^3 = x^3 + 3x^2\;dx + 3x\;(dx)^2 + (dx)^3.

Ignoring the higher-order terms, we have:

(x+dx)3−x3≈3x2  dx,(x+dx)^3 - x^3 \approx 3x^2\;dx,

so that

x2  dx≈(x+dx)3−x33.x^2\;dx \approx \frac{(x+dx)^3 - x^3}{3}.
  • The subsequent equations follow the same idea.

2. Adjusting the Difference Equations for a Finite Increment ΔX\Delta X

If instead of an infinitesimal dxdx we have a finite increment ΔX\Delta X, the exact equations include higher-order terms. For example, using the binomial expansion we obtain:

x  ΔX+(ΔX)22=(x+ΔX)2−x22,x2  ΔX+x  (ΔX)2+(ΔX)33=(x+ΔX)3−x33,x3  ΔX+3x2  (ΔX)22+x  (ΔX)3+(ΔX)42=(x+ΔX)4−x44.\begin{array}{ll} x\;\Delta X + \frac{(\Delta X)^2}{2} = \frac{(x + \Delta X)^2 - x^2}{2}, \\[1mm] x^2\;\Delta X + x\;(\Delta X)^2 + \frac{(\Delta X)^3}{3} = \frac{(x + \Delta X)^3 - x^3}{3}, \\[1mm] x^3\;\Delta X + \frac{3x^2\;(\Delta X)^2}{2} + x\;(\Delta X)^3 + \frac{(\Delta X)^4}{2} = \frac{(x + \Delta X)^4 - x^4}{4}. \end{array}

Explanation

  • In the first equation, the full expansion is:
(x+ΔX)2=x2+2x  ΔX+(ΔX)2.(x + \Delta X)^2 = x^2 + 2x\;\Delta X + (\Delta X)^2.

Hence,

(x+ΔX)2−x22=2x  ΔX+(ΔX)22=x  ΔX+(ΔX)22.\frac{(x + \Delta X)^2 - x^2}{2} = \frac{2x\;\Delta X + (\Delta X)^2}{2} = x\;\Delta X + \frac{(\Delta X)^2}{2}.
  • The higher equations are obtained by applying the binomial theorem to (x+ΔX)k(x + \Delta X)^k and then subtracting xkx^k.

These equations illustrate how, when the increment is not infinitesimally small, additional terms appear to account for the full change.

3. Application: Deriving the Antiderivative of cos⁡(x)\cos(x)

Let us now see how these difference equations connect with integration.

3.1 Integration of cos⁡(x)\cos(x)

We aim to show:

∫cos⁡(x)  dx=sin⁡(x)+C.\int \cos(x) \; dx = \sin(x) + C.

Step 1. Express the Increment in sin⁡(x)\sin(x)From the sine addition formula,

sin⁡(x+Δx)=sin⁡(x)cos⁡(Δx)+cos⁡(x)sin⁡(Δx).\sin(x + \Delta x) = \sin(x)\cos(\Delta x) + \cos(x)\sin(\Delta x).

For very small Δx\Delta x, we use the approximations:

cos⁡(Δx)≈1andsin⁡(Δx)≈Δx.\cos(\Delta x) \approx 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \sin(\Delta x) \approx \Delta x.

Thus,

sin⁡(x+Δx)≈sin⁡(x)+cos⁡(x)Δx.\sin(x + \Delta x) \approx \sin(x) + \cos(x) \Delta x.

Subtract sin⁡(x)\sin(x) from both sides:

sin⁡(x+Δx)−sin⁡(x)≈cos⁡(x)Δx.\sin(x + \Delta x) - \sin(x) \approx \cos(x)\Delta x.

Step 2. Forming the Telescoping SumDivide the interval [0,π][0, \pi] into subintervals of width Δx\Delta x. Then

∫0πcos⁡(x)  dx≈∑i=0nΔxcos⁡(iΔx)  Δx.\int_{0}^{\pi} \cos(x)\; dx \approx \sum_{i=0}^{\frac{n}{\Delta x}} \cos(i\Delta x) \; \Delta x.

But as we just observed, for each subinterval the change in sin⁡(x)\sin(x) is approximately:

sin⁡((i+1)Δx)−sin⁡(iΔx)≈cos⁡(iΔx)Δx.\sin((i+1)\Delta x) - \sin(i\Delta x) \approx \cos(i\Delta x)\Delta x.

Thus, the sum becomes:

∑i=0nΔx[sin⁡((i+1)Δx)−sin⁡(iΔx)].\sum_{i=0}^{\frac{n}{\Delta x}} \left[\sin((i+1)\Delta x) - \sin(i\Delta x)\right].

This is a telescoping series; almost every term cancels:

sin⁡(Δx)−sin⁡(0)+sin⁡(2Δx)−sin⁡(Δx)+...+sin⁡(N)−sin⁡(N−Δx)=sin⁡(N)−sin⁡(0).\sin(\Delta x) - \sin(0) + \sin(2\Delta x) - \sin(\Delta x) + ... + \sin(N) - \sin(N-\Delta x) = \sin(N) - \sin(0).

Taking the limit as Δx→0\Delta x \to 0 , we deduce:

∫0πcos⁡(x)  dx=sin⁡(π)−sin⁡(0)=0−0=0.\int_{0}^{\pi} \cos(x)\; dx = \sin(\pi) - \sin(0) = 0 - 0 = 0.

(For the general antiderivative, one obtains ∫cos⁡(x)  dx=sin⁡(x)+C\int \cos(x)\; dx = \sin(x) + C.)

4. Application: Deriving the Antiderivative of sin⁡(x)\sin(x)

Similarly, consider:

∫sin⁡(x)  dx.\int \sin(x)\; dx.

Step 1. Express the Increment in cos⁡(x)\cos(x)Recall the cosine addition formula:

cos⁡(x+Δx)=cos⁡(x)cos⁡(Δx)−sin⁡(x)sin⁡(Δx).\cos(x + \Delta x) = \cos(x)\cos(\Delta x) - \sin(x)\sin(\Delta x).

For small Δx\Delta x, this approximates to:

cos⁡(x+Δx)≈cos⁡(x)−sin⁡(x)Δx.\cos(x + \Delta x) \approx \cos(x) - \sin(x)\Delta x.

Rearrange to obtain:

cos⁡(x)−cos⁡(x+Δx)≈sin⁡(x)Δx.\cos(x) - \cos(x + \Delta x) \approx \sin(x)\Delta x.

Thus,

sin⁡(x)Δx≈cos⁡(x)−cos⁡(x+Δx).\sin(x)\Delta x \approx \cos(x) - \cos(x+\Delta x).

Step 2. Telescoping SumDivide the interval and sum:

∑i=0n/Δx[cos⁡(iΔx)−cos⁡((i+1)Δx)]\sum_{i=0}^{n/\Delta x} \left[\cos(i\Delta x) - \cos((i+1)\Delta x)\right]

telescopes to:

cos⁡(0)−cos⁡(nΔx).\cos(0) - \cos(n\Delta x).

Taking the limit as Δx→0\Delta x \to 0 (and adjusting for the proper interval), we deduce:

∫sin⁡(x)  dx=−cos⁡(x)+C.\int \sin(x)\; dx = -\cos(x) + C.

5. Application: Deriving the Antiderivative of 1x\frac{1}{x}

Using the logarithm, note the following expansion from previous work:

log⁡(x+h)=log⁡(x)+log⁡ ⁣((1+hx)xh)hx=log⁡(x)+log⁡ ⁣(ehx)=log⁡(x)+hx.\begin{array}{ll} \log(x+h) = \log(x) + \log\!\Bigl( \Bigl(1+\frac{h}{x}\Bigr)^{\frac{x}{h}} \Bigr)^{\frac{h}{x}} \\ = \log(x) + \log\!\Bigl(e^{\frac{h}{x}}\Bigr) \\ = \log(x) + \frac{h}{x}. \end{array}

Thus,

hx=log⁡(x+h)−log⁡(x).\frac{h}{x} = \log(x+h) - \log(x).

When you sum these differences over an interval, the telescoping nature gives:

∫1x  dx=ln⁡∣x∣+C.\int \frac{1}{x}\;dx = \ln|x| + C.

6. Integration by Parts (A Brief Mention)

An additional powerful tool is integration by parts, which can also be derived using difference equations. For example, if we write the expansion for functions f(x)f(x) and G(x)G(x) (with G′(x)=g(x)G'(x) = g(x)) as:

f(x+Δx)=A0+A1  Δx+A2  (Δx)2+...,G(x+Δx)=B0+B1  Δx+B2  (Δx)2+...,\begin{array}{ll} f(x+\Delta x) = A_0 + A_1\;\Delta x + A_2\;(\Delta x)^2 + ..., \\ G(x+\Delta x) = B_0 + B_1\;\Delta x + B_2\;(\Delta x)^2 + ..., \end{array}

then the product f(x)G(x)f(x)G(x) has a difference that telescopes. The careful grouping of terms ultimately leads to the familiar integration by parts formula:

∫u  dv=uv−∫v  du.\boxed{\int u\;dv = uv - \int v\;du.}

7. Conclusion

We have examined how difference equations can be used to “reverse-engineer” antiderivatives by considering the differences between successive values of a function. By writing:

x  dx=(x+dx)2−x22,x2  dx=(x+dx)3−x33,x3  dx=(x+dx)4−x44,x4  dx=(x+dx)5−x55,\begin{array}{ll} x\;dx = \frac{(x+dx)^2 - x^2}{2},\\[1mm] x^2\;dx = \frac{(x+dx)^3 - x^3}{3},\\[1mm] x^3\;dx = \frac{(x+dx)^4 - x^4}{4},\\[1mm] x^4\;dx = \frac{(x+dx)^5 - x^5}{5}, \end{array}

we see a clear pattern: the differential (or the infinitesimal change) times a power of xx equals the difference of two successive higher powers of xx, divided by a constant.

By summing these differences over a partition, we obtain telescoping sums that yield the net change in the antiderivative. This process provides a natural and intuitive derivation of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, as well as alternative derivations for integration formulas (for cos⁡(x)\cos(x), sin⁡(x)\sin(x), 1x\frac{1}{x}, and even integration by parts).

This approach emphasizes the importance of algebraic patterns and finite differences, reinforcing the idea that integration is simply the accumulation of infinitesimal changes. By understanding and working through these difference equations step by step, one gains a deeper insight into the structure of antiderivatives and the fundamental connections between discrete sums and continuous change.
